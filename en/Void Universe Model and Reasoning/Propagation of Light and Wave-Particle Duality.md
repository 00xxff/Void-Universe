
### Propagation of Light and Wave-Particle Duality

#### What is Light? How Does It Propagate?

It is known that:

1. Light is a type of electromagnetic wave.
2. The speed of light in any inertial reference frame within the same medium is constant.
3. Light exhibits wave-particle duality.

Now, we will attempt to use the Void Model to analyze the principles of light propagation and the cause of its wave-particle duality.

Firstly, starting with the constant speed of light $(c)$, in our everyday understanding, mechanical waves can maintain a constant propagation speed within the same medium. Since light is an electromagnetic wave, it is certain that it possesses wave characteristics. Secondly, the frequency and wavelength of light determine its speed, meaning that within the same medium of an inertial reference frame, all types of light and electromagnetic waves must adhere to the law $(\lambda \times f = c)$. This is something that pure probability waves cannot achieve, leading us to reject the hypothesis that light consists of photons forming a probability wave in particle form. Therefore, we assume that light and electromagnetic waves are forms of waves.

For a wave to propagate, it requires a medium, which must exhibit varying densities to reasonably explain the changes in light's propagation speed across different media.

In our Void Model, there exists such a medium — void particles, a medium that permeates the entire void. In regions where mass accumulates, the density of void particles increases toward the center of mass. This aligns with the phenomenon of gravitational lensing, where light paths bend or deflect as they pass by massive celestial bodies. In various material media composed of different densities, like air, water, and glass, the density of void particles also increases proportionally with the material's density, corresponding inversely to the speed of light in these media.

Therefore, in our Void Model, void particles fully satisfy the macroscopic behavior expected of a medium for light propagation.

Next, we will delve into the microscopic analysis of the principles behind the generation and propagation of light, as well as why it exhibits wave-particle duality.

From the above reasoning, we have determined that light propagates as a wave through void particles. But how does this wave form and propagate?

Thus, the propagation of electromagnetic waves in a medium requires this kind of omnipresent, uniform, and constant oscillating centripetal force throughout the void.

We find confirmation of this in the formation of the universe and the creation of void pressure. This alternating transdimensional oscillation forms electromagnetic waves.
